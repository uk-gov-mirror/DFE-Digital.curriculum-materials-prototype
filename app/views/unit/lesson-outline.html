Lesson 1: types of volcano

Lesson Summary

Objective: to learn the types of volcano

By the end of the lesson pupils should:

  • know the characteristics of different types of volcanoes, and distinguish one type from another based on its eruption style
  • be able to locate volcanoes on a map using grid coordinates

Core knowledge for all pupils

Pupils should know that:

  • volcanoes occur at a destructive plate boundary
  • a lava flow is molten rock only
  • a pyroclastic flow is a mixture of molten rock, ash and gases

Vocabulary for this learning objective

Pupils should know the words:

  • cone
  • vent
  • magma chamber
  • dome
  • shield
  • strato

Misconceptions to address

The following misconceptions should be addressed:

  • all volcanoes erupt violently (this misconception arises from media coverage which is biased by location and death tolls)
  • if a volcano doesn’t erupt for a hundred years, it’s extinct (in fact, the volcano is probably just dormant)
  • volcanoes only form on land, not under water (actually, there are numerous oceanic fault lines with volcanoes forming along these without breaking the surface of the water)
  • magma comes from deep within the Earth’s mantle (this comes from textbooks’ saying that the magma is ‘deep’ down - in reality, virtually all magma originates in the upper portion of the mantle and crust because of plate friction)

Building on previous knowledge

Vocabulary

Pupils should already know:

  • lava: red-hot liquid rock
  • volcano: a mountain that erupts, or has erupted
  • volcanic eruption: an explosion or outpouring of lava, rock or as from a volcano

Core knowledge for teachers

The impacts of volcanic eruptions are categorised into primary and secondary effects, as well as: social, economic, and environmental.

Primary effects

Direct impacts of a volcanic hazard (for example, deaths and destruction).

Secondary effects

The result of another effect, usually a primary effect (for example, homelessness).

Social effects

Effects that impact people.

Economic effects

Effects that impact the economy of a location or people’s access to income.

Environmental effects

Effects that impact the natural world (for example, nature or the topography of the landscape).

The effects of a volcanic eruption can continue to develop over a series of days or sometimes weeks depending on the timeline and strength of the eruption.
